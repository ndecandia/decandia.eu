Untamed Zimbabwe

A 4x4 journey through a country that turns its back on tourism — and rewards the few who arrive anyway.

Style Self-driven, wild camp
Distance ~4,000 km
Best season Dry months

The country

A place that hides on purpose

Zimbabwe is one of those countries that the maps know but the world has forgotten. The roads are few and the lodges fewer; what passes for a national park is sometimes a faded sign at the edge of a track. And yet, perhaps for that reason, it stays with you.

You will rarely see another western traveller. The villages are small, dignified, ferociously hospitable. The wildlife — especially the elephants — appears in numbers that feel almost prehistoric. The wide spaces are silent in a way that European silence never is.

This is not a country to consume. It is a country to enter slowly, in a vehicle you trust, with enough water, enough fuel, and a tolerance for dust that becomes part of your skin by day three.

Highlights

What stays in the eye

Eight places, threaded together by long red tracks and the kind of horizons that bend the day.

Hwange

An immense, sun-cracked park where the elephants seem to outnumber the trees. Sleep at a quiet bush camp by a waterhole and let the night come to you.

Matobo

No big game here, and that is the point. Granite domes piled like sleeping animals, ancient rock paintings, and a horizon broken by stone instead of bush.

Great Zimbabwe

The dry-stone capital that gave the country its name. Walk the walls slowly: there is more here than the guidebooks tell.

Gonarezhou

The wildest of Zimbabwe's parks. The northern half is river country — the Chilojo cliffs in late light are the kind of view that makes you stop talking.

Victoria Falls

Loud, wet, undeniable. Yes, it is touristy — but the noise of a million litres falling at once does not negotiate.

Matusadona & Lake Kariba

A drowned forest, half-skeletal trees standing in still water. Few animals, fewer people, and sunsets that take their time.

Kubu Island

Just over the border in Botswana, a granite island marooned in a salt pan. Climb to the top before sunrise. Bring a beer for sundown.

Hunter's Road

An old track running along the Zimbabwe-Botswana border, used by no-one and watched by no-one. Pure savannah, pure silence. GPS only — the road is not on any map.

The route

How the days fall

A loose backbone of the journey. Most of it is dust; the asphalt is the boring part.

01

Arrival in Johannesburg

Pick up the vehicles, an unhurried first lunch, and a long drive north toward the border country. The first night is usually a guesthouse — a soft landing before the camping begins.

02

Crossing into Botswana — the salt pans

Through Martin's Drift and across the Makgadikgadi to Kubu Island, a place that feels like the world's quiet attic. The tracks here are obvious enough; the sense of scale is what surprises you.

03

Elephant Sands & the Hunter's Road

A waterhole that the elephants treat as a living room, then a day on the unmarked Hunter's Road along the border. Vegetation thickens, then opens; you may see no human all day.

04

Into Zimbabwe — Hwange

The park is enormous. The northern camps (Sinamatella, Masuma) are the soul of it; the south is rougher, lonelier, and worth the discomfort. Sleep at a bush camp on a pan if you can — you will not regret it.

05

Bulawayo & Matobo

A short return to fuel, food and signal in Bulawayo, then south to the granite world of Matobo. Camp inside the park if you can; the night sky here is unfair.

06

Great Zimbabwe & Gonarezhou

A morning at the ancient walls, then a long push east to the river country. The Chipinda Pools camps are right on the water; the hippos provide the soundtrack.

07

Crossing south — Beitbridge to Pretoria

The last long drive. The border at Beitbridge is the only true crowd of the trip. After that, asphalt, a final asado, and the slow re-entry into noise.

Variants

The same country, three ways

This route has been walked more than once, and the country has obliged differently each time. A few alternative threads, in case you want to weave your own.

The Botswana approach

The classic version: enter Zimbabwe from the west, through Botswana and the salt pans, and exit south through Gonarezhou. The longest, the wildest, and the one with the strongest sense of crossing into a forgotten place.

The Kruger swap

If the rains find you, Gonarezhou can become impassable. The fall-back is to skip south into South Africa and finish in Kruger. You lose the wildness; you gain the wild dogs.

All Zimbabwe, with the falls

A purely Zimbabwean loop, anti-clockwise, that adds Victoria Falls and the still water of Matusadona on Lake Kariba. Less Botswana, more variety, and an extra day on the road for it.

What to expect

The shape of the days

Tents every night with a few exceptions. Cooking on a fire, washing in tepid water, and a long, dusty driving day between camps. Fuel stations are rare in Zimbabwe; jerrycans filled in Botswana save a lot of anxiety later.

A GPS with pre-loaded tracks is not a luxury. Many of the most beautiful stretches — Hunter's Road, the southern half of Hwange, the Matobo back-country, the south of Gonarezhou — are simply not on standard maps. Without tracks you can still see the country; you will just see the easier half of it.

Bring patience for borders, small bills for small bribes, and a willingness to share food at the campfire with whoever wanders over. That, in the end, is most of what the trip turns out to be about.
